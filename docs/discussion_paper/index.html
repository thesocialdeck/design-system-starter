Introduction Link to section

The purpose of this discussion paper is to support the Australian Government’s consultations for the 2020 Review of the Disability Standards for Education. In this paper, we explain what the Standards are and what they do, the purpose of the 2020 Review, what previous reviews told us, and how you can get involved. The paper also includes discussion questions to help us find out your views and experiences.

Accessing and participating in education are key to enabling people with disability to participate fully in society and maximise their opportunities. Positive education experiences have a profound impact on an individual’s future and can lead to further study, employment and a rewarding life.

In the 2018 ABS Survey of Disability, Ageing and Carers (SDAC), 4 per cent of 0-4 year olds, 10 per cent of 5-19 year olds and 13 per cent of 15-64 year olds reported as having disability. Overall, people with disability made up 17.7 per cent of the Australian population in 2018.

Further, in 2019, nearly one in five (19.9 per cent) of school students across Australia received an adjustment due to disability according to the Nationally Consistent Collection of Data on School Students with Disability (NCCD).

Role of the Disability Standards for EducationLink to section

The Disability Standards for Education 2005 (the Standards) came into effect on 18 August 2005. They seek to ensure that students with disability can access and participate in education on the same basis as other students.

The Standards are subordinate legislation to the Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (the DDA). Under the DDA, it is unlawful to discriminate against a person because of a disability. The DDA protects people with disability against discrimination in many areas of public life, including education.

Under the DDA, the Attorney-General may make Disability Standards to specify rights and responsibilities about equal access and opportunity for people with a disability in more detail and with more certainty than the DDA itself provides.

The following Standards have been made under the DDA:

  • Disability Standards for Accessible Public Transport 2002

  • Disability Standards for Education 2005

  • Disability (Access to Premises – Buildings) Standards 2010.

If a person acts in accordance with the Disability Standards for Education 2005, they comply with the DDA. An education provider must comply with the Standards or it will be acting unlawfully.

Purpose of the Disability Standards for EducationLink to section

The purpose of the Standards is to clarify the education provisions of the DDA. This is intended to make it easier to understand the rights and obligations under the DDA.

The Standards cover enrolment, participation, curriculum development, accreditation and delivery, student support services and elimination of harassment and victimisation.

Each part of the Standards sets out:

  • the rights of students with disability in relation to education and training to help people understand what is fair and reasonable under the Standards

  • the legal obligations or responsibilities of education providers

  • the measures of compliance that are examples of what can be done to meet the requirements of each part of the Standards.

The Standards provide detail about an education provider’s obligation to make reasonable adjustments to assist a student with disability to participate in education on the same basis as other students. There is no obligation to make an unreasonable adjustment.

Education providers must consult with students and their family members or carers about reasonable adjustments. They also take into account the interests of affected people such as staff and other students, and they ensure the integrity of the course or education program and its assessment is maintained.

The Standards also clarify circumstances where an education provider is exempted from making a reasonable adjustment where it would impose an unjustifiable hardship on them. The exemption does not apply to addressing harassment and victimisation.

Education providers covered by the Standards includes preschools and kindergartens, government and private schools, TAFEs and other vocational education and training providers, and higher education institutions.

If a person believes an education provider is not complying with the Standards, they have the right to make a complaint to the Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC) about disability discrimination. The AHRC will try to resolve the situation through a conciliation process.

In 2018–19, the AHRC received 891 complaints under the DDA.[1] Of those, 155 related to education (13 per cent), making it the third highest area of complaints, followed by 115 complaints regarding disability standards (10 per cent).[2]

If the AHRC conciliation is unsuccessful, an aggrieved person may commence legal proceedings in the Federal Court or Federal Magistrate’s Court.

2020 Review of the Standards Link to section

The Federal Minister for Education is required to review the Standards every five years in consultation with the Attorney-General. The Standards are due for review in 2020. Previous reviews were in 2010 and 2015.

The review tests if the Standards are effective in achieving their objects, which are:

(a) to eliminate, as far as possible, discrimination against persons on the ground of disability in the area of education and training; and

(b) to ensure, as far as practicable, that persons with disabilities have the same rights to equality before the law in the area of education and training as the rest of the community; and

(c) to promote recognition and acceptance within the community of the principle that persons with disabilities have the same fundamental rights as the rest of the community.

Essentially, the Review asks whether the Standards are doing their job and, if not, how they could be improved.

Scope and focus of the 2020 ReviewLink to section

To understand whether the Standards are doing their job, we need to hear the everyday experiences of students, their families and carers, their education providers, their teachers and educators and their advocates.

We also need to talk to states and territories, education authorities, national agencies and regulators, and representatives of the non-government sector.

Using the knowledge and understanding we gain, we can then determine how well the Standards are working as the mechanism for interpreting the DDA for education and training provision.

Terms of reference for the 2020 Review

In considering the effectiveness of the Standards, the Review will consider the following:

  1. Are the rights, obligations and measures of compliance set out in the Standards (and its Guidance Notes) clear and appropriate?

  2. Do students, families, educators, education providers and policy makers know about, understand, apply and comply with the rights, obligations and measures of compliance in the Standards?

  3. In the 15 years since the Standards were developed, have the Standards contributed towards students with disability being able to access education and training opportunities on the same basis as students without disabilities?

Consultation for the Review will include a focus on the impact of the Standards on the experiences of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students with disability.

In formulating recommendations, the Review will focus on opportunities for national collaborative action between jurisdictions and agencies, including action to strengthen the knowledge and capabilities of teachers and educators, and whether any amendments to the Standards should be made.

Scope of the ReviewLink to section

We recognise that there are broader discussions about the best way to provide education for students with disability, which have been aired in many reviews and inquiries, and continue to be explored through the Royal Commission into Violence, Abuse, Neglect and Exploitation of People with Disability (the Disability Royal Commission). We acknowledge that there are strong views about issues like inclusive education and the existence of special schools or segregated settings, as well as funding for students with disability. It is important to note that some of these issues are outside the scope of this Review.

The threshold question for this Review is whether and to what extent the Standards are making a positive difference towards students with disability being able to access education and training opportunities on the same basis as students without disability. To answer that question we need your help.

The 2010 and 2015 ReviewsLink to section

The 2010 Review found the Standards were a good framework for promoting access and participation in education but there were a number of issues identified that were seen to weaken the effectiveness of the Standards. The 2015 Review found that whilst there been significant improvement in the accessibility and use of the Standards since 2010, it was evident that further effort and support tools were still required.

Key themes identified in both the 2010 and 2015 Reviews of the Standards include:

  • awareness raising – more needed to be done to ensure that the Standards were promoted widely, were accessible to all and were well understood

  • clarity and definitions – terms such as ‘reasonable adjustments’ and ‘unjustifiable hardship’ were not being universally applied and were not interpreted consistently across different jurisdictions and sectors

  • complaints – the handling of complaints, especially given the requirement for families to engage early with the legal system, given limited advice and systems in place to handle complaints (including negotiation and arbitration processes)

  • accountability – individuals found that not all providers demonstrated a willingness to be accountable and to acknowledge both the rights of individuals and their own obligations under the Standards

  • compliance – there was feedback about situations where Standards were not being fully complied with, some of which stemmed from a poor understanding of the obligations of the Standards.

We anticipate the consultations for the 2020 Review will find similar themes and we will look at the extent of progress made to address these areas since 2015, including significant national and state reforms to support students with disability.

We acknowledge some recommendations from the 2015 Review have not been progressed. We will consider which remain relevant as part of the current Review, and which have been superseded.

How to get involved in the 2020 ReviewLink to section

We welcome comments on any aspect of the Standards from anyone who would like to make a contribution. This includes young people still in the education system right through to those who advocate for others – we want to hear from as many people as possible.

All contributions will build our understanding of your experiences and help us work out if changes need to be made to support effectiveness of the Standards and what these changes should be.

We are particularly interested in hearing the voices of young people and knowing more about the experiences of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students with disability and their families.

Our public consultations will run over a three-month period from July 2020. We will offer you a range of ways to get involved and have your story heard.

This includes making written, video and audio submissions and through a variety of online consultation opportunities.

Our website will host an active discussion forum, webinars and a survey if you would like to make a contribution in that way.

The COVID-19 pandemic has meant that most consultation will be online, however we will undertake some face to face consultation for specific groups if that becomes possible.

More information on how you can get involved will be available on the Review website and you can sign up to receive updates as more information becomes available.

Tell us your story Link to section

We welcome comments on any aspect of the Standards and offer the following questions as discussion starters to help you with telling us your story, whether through writing a submission or contributing in other ways to the Review.

While we have grouped questions into broad categories, we welcome your response to any question from any category. They are simply a guide to help explain the sort of information and experiences we are interested in hearing about.

When you contribute to the Review, please let us know whether you are a student, parent / carer, educator, advocate, or an education provider. Please also let us know to which education sector your comments relate, e.g. preschool, schooling, VET, university. Of course, general comments about your whole of life education experience are also welcome.

Questions for students and parents or carers

Clarity and scope of the Standards

  • Are the Standards easy to understand?

  • Do the terms used in the Standards make sense?

  • Do the Standards make it easier to understand the rights and obligations under the DDA?

  • Do any parts of the Standards need changing to make them clearer?

  • Are there any gaps in the Standards?

Awareness, understanding and compliance

  • Do you know you / your child has a legal right to enrol, access support and participate in education and training on the same basis as other students?

  • Do you know your education provider has a legal responsibility to take steps to prevent harassment or victimisation of a student with disability?

  • Where did you find out about the Standards?

  • Do the Standards help you to understand your / your child’s rights?

  • Do you believe education providers know about and understand their legal responsibilities under the Standards and the DDA?

  • What should be done to improve awareness of the Standards and their explanation of rights and obligations under the DDA?

Experiences of students with disability and their parents

  • What has been your / your child’s experience of accessing and participating in education and training? Was this different during COVID-19? It’s OK to provide just one example here, or share several stories.

  • Do education and training providers know about the barriers students with disability may face accessing education and training?

  • Tell us about your experience with enrolment and access – for example, what was the process like, were you happy with the outcome?

  • Tell us about your experience with reasonable adjustments – for example, what was the process like, how did your education provider consult with you, were you happy with the outcome?

  • Tell us if you have experienced harassment or victimisation in an education setting – for example, what happened, what steps did your education provider take to address this?

  • If you believed your education provider was not meeting their obligations, how was this resolved? Did you know how to make a complaint? What happened?

Impact of the Standards

  • Do you think the Standards help students with disability to access and participate in education and training on the same basis as other students? Was this different during COVID-19?

  • How do you think the Standards could be improved to help students with disability?

  • What barriers remain for students with disability wanting to access and participate in education and training? How could the Standards be changed to assist or address these barriers?

Questions for educators and providers of education and training

Clarity and scope of the Standards

  • Are the Standards easy to understand?

  • Do the terms used in the Standards make sense?

  • Do the Standards make it easier to understand the rights and obligations under the DDA?

  • Do any parts of the Standards need changing to make them clearer?

  • Are there any gaps in the Standards?

Awareness, understanding and compliance

  • Do you know a student with disability has a legal right to enrol, access support and participate in education and training on the same basis as other students?

  • Do you know educators and education providers have a legal responsibility to take steps to prevent harassment or victimisation of a student with disability?

  • How, and how often, do you use the Standards?

  • Do the Standards help you to understand the rights of students and your obligations as educators and providers?

  • If you are an educator, what information and support is made available to you about the Standards? If you are an education provider, what information and support do you provide to students and educators about the Standards?

  • What could be done to improve awareness of the Standards and their explanation of rights and obligations under the DDA?

  • To what extent do the Standards assist you to understand and comply with your responsibilities under the DDA?

  • Do you consider that students and their parents know enough about how to resolve an issue if things go wrong? What could be adjusted to improve complaints processes?

Experiences of educators and providers

  • What has been your experience of students with disability accessing and participating in education and training? Was this different during COVID-19? It’s OK to provide just one example here, or share several stories.

  • How much do you know about the barriers students with disability may face accessing education and training?

  • Have there been situations where you were unclear what was expected of you under the Standards?

Impact of the Standards

  • Do you think the Standards have made it easier for students with disability to access and participate in education and training? Was this different during COVID-19?

  • How do you think the Standards could be improved to help students with disability?

  • What barriers remain for students with disability wanting to access and participate in education and training? How could the Standards be changed to assist or address these barriers?

Early childhood education and the StandardsLink to section

In an early childhood education and care context, the Standards currently apply to preschools and kindergartens, but specifically exclude childcare providers.

Both the 2010 and 2015 Reviews found that there was some confusion in the early childhood education and care sector about expectations and responsibilities. The Reviews both recommended considering extending the application of the Standards to childcare providers.

Officials from Commonwealth and state and territory governments have undertaken work considering the impact and effects of a change to the Standards, and the extent to which it would support greater clarity and understanding of rights and obligations under the DDA in respect to early childhood education and care settings.

Considerations around potential extension of the Standards to other early childhood education and care settings will be tested through this Review. A separate discussion paper will be published in August seeking your experiences related to the Standards and the DDA in early childhood education and care and your views on the way forward.

Public consultations will inform our advice to governmentsLink to section

Your input to the Review will provide insights into the extent the Standards are or are not meeting their stated objectives, and are working for students in their everyday lives. Stories of your experience will be important in forming these insights.

If we find that the Standards are not currently working well, or where areas of improvement could be made, we will be making recommendations on what needs to change, how to change it, and in what timeframe changes should be implemented.

We will do this in partnership with states and territories and will be working closely and collaboratively in developing the final report and associated report recommendations. Our intention is to focus on opportunities for national collaborative action, as recommendations will likely require individual or joint action from the Australian Government, states and territories, and both government and non-government education providers.

Once the report has been finalised, we will present it to all governments for their consideration and decisions on implementation of associated recommendations.
